#Mountain Geomorphology
My research engages with the geomorphology of mountainous environments, with a particular focus on the escarpments and high-altitude landscapes of southern Africa. I am interested in how elevation, lithology, and climate interact to drive erosional and depositional processes, and how these dynamics shape the distinctive landforms found across montane settings such as the Drakensberg and associated Great Escarpment.

#Remote Sensing in Geomorphological Research
I apply remote sensing techniques to investigate surface processes and landform change at scales that are difficult to access through conventional fieldwork. This includes the use of satellite-derived data — including radar and optical imagery — to detect and monitor geomorphological activity, with particular interest in Sentinel-1 SAR data and interferometric approaches for surface deformation analysis.

#Differential SAR Interferometry (DInSAR)
A specific focus of my remote sensing work involves Differential Synthetic Aperture Radar Interferometry (DInSAR) as a tool for detecting subtle surface displacements in mountainous terrain. I am interested in how phase-difference information derived from multi-temporal SAR acquisitions can be used to identify mass movement, periglacial activity, and other geomorphological phenomena across complex, high-relief landscapes.

#GIS-Based Geomorphological Mapping
I use Geographic Information Systems (GIS) as a core analytical platform for geomorphological mapping and terrain analysis. This includes the derivation and application of geomorphometric indices — such as the Stream Length-Gradient Index, Hypsometric Integral, and related morphotectonic measures — to characterise landscape form, identify structural controls, and map geomorphological units at regional and local scales.

#Landscape Evolution
My research focuses on understanding how landscapes have evolved over geological and historical timescales in response to tectonic, climatic, and fluvial forcing. I am particularly interested in the long-term incision histories of mountain catchments, the preservation of relict landforms, and the interplay between resistant lithologies and differential erosion in shaping the current configuration of southern African escarpment landscapes.

#Dating of Landscape Features
Constraining the timing of landscape-forming events is central to reconstructing geomorphological histories. My interest in geochronology focuses on applying relative and numerical dating techniques to features such as alluvial fans, debris deposits, and periglacial landforms. This includes the use of palaeoenvironmental proxies and, where applicable, exposure dating approaches to place landform development within broader regional climate and tectonic narratives
